Milton's Satan Secret

The center-stage epic hero role that Satan plays in Milton's Paradise Lost, has been controversial for centuries. In some ways, it almost looks like Milton is glorifying Satan in his attempt to take revenge upon God. However, as a Christian theologian, it would be unlikely that Milton would take this heretical view. So, the answer must be more complex.


In the writing of Paradise Lost, Milton sets out to create an epic written in English heroic verse, analogous to the Virgil's Latin Aeneid, and Homer's Greek Iliad and Odyssey (“The Verse”, Paradise Lost). Though attempting to emulate the works of the great Classics, Milton makes it clear in his call to the “heavenly muse”, the Holy Spirit, that his work will “soar above the Aonian mount, while it pursues things unattempted yet in prose or rhyme” (Book I, Lines 14-16). The Holy Spirit as his guide, Milton claims that his confidence to surpass any epic poet comes from “thou, O Spirit that dost prefer before all temples the upright heart and pure” (Book I, Lines 17-18).


In most traditional epics, the main character is the hero. The Odyssey has its Odysseus, and the Aeneid has its Aeneas. In Milton's epic, most of the time is focused around the actions of Satan, as he attempts to avenge God's judgment against his rebellion by thwarting the perfection of his creation. Instead of being portrayed as a weak, ineffective character, Satan is proud, clever, and very capable of leading an army. In many ways he embodies the traditional heroic ideals. Like many Greek heros, he is a military general.


This leads to a problem, because Satan cannot be a hero in Milton's epic if his actions and motives are morally bad. In a move of pride, he tries to ascend to glory equal to that of God, but he and his followers are cast out (Book I). Satan is also fully responsible for bringing Sin and Death into the world (Book II), and the fall of Man (Book IX). We are left with an evil character being portrayed as a hero (Murphy).


The conflicting depiction of a bad Satan, but a good hero, has confused critics over the centuries. According to Christopher Murphy, a researcher at the University of California, most interpretations of Satan's character separate his person from his poetic construction. C.S Lewis' explanation, in his Preface to Paradise Lost, claims that Satan's actions demonstrate an ineffectiveness that negates his heroism. Some critics, including Stanley Fish, claim that Satan's heroism directly controverts his evil. Other critics will make the claim that Satan is not a hero at all, or a perversion of the classic hero (Murphy).


In some ways, these explanations may satisfy the awkward tension between Satan as a hero, and Satan as a wicked corrupter of mankind and arch enemy of God. After all, no realistic hero is without his or her internal conflicts. However, this does not explain Milton's choice of Satan to be the hero and main focus of his book. Instead of long, eloquently drawn out scenes before the throne of God, we are treated to long, drawn out speeches where Satan is praising himself, and gloating over victories against God that he is planning to win (Book I, Book II). Nowhere are there any scenes in heaven, where angels are flustered, dressing themselves for war. In the few scenes where we are shown God's celestial glory, God is discussing the danger of Satan. Nor are the other protagonists of the book, Adam and Eve, given long sections to air their feelings and praise God's love and the beauty of the world, or marvel upon the wonder of being alive.


It seems strange that Milton would choose to pick Satan as the hero and main character for a “Christian” epic. Could Milton's choice of Satan as a hero be a perversion of the classical epic? This explanation also seems to be unlikely because of Milton's almost religious attachment to the epic form in every other way. It seems to be then, that Milton's depiction of Satan must be allegorical in purpose.


Milton uses Paradise Lost as a stand for preaching many of his own personal opinions. In his struggles for a happy marriage, he makes the claim that women are morally lower than men, and more susceptible to temptation (Book IX). Milton also makes the contrary stand (for his time) against traditional Calvinism, refuting his peers in claiming that Man had free will. It seems that Milton's portrayal of Satan is an allegory, and Miltons uses the pedestal of his literary power to suggest his point.


From Milton's point of view, Jesus would be the real hero, but he gravitates away from the spotlight because He is humble. Milton demonstrates Jesus' humility when he compares the God's providing of clothes for Adam and Eve to Jesus' washing of his disciples feet (Lines 211-217, Book X). Because the Holy Spirit is “inspiring” the work, the role of God is one of humility and justice. Milton must have thought that Jesus didn't want to be the super-star of his work. Milton's portrayal of Satan figuratively represents the glory that the ancient epics tried to accomplish. Both Virgil and Homer put an emphasis of creating big heroes who despite the odds, managed to overcome their circumstances and bring glory to themselves. Milton realizes that the glory of an epic (and its heroes), even ones as revered as the Aeneid and the Odyssey are only temporary.


In addition to their relatively ephemeral nature, neither the pagan heroes nor the pagan authors give credit to where credit is due: God. Milton's reverence for Satan is a reflection of his reverence for the Greek epics, because the epics are great and powerful like Satan. There is nothing wrong with beauty and power, but if it is not used towards glorifying God, than the talents that God has gifted that individual have been misguided and are lost. This explains why Milton can glorify Satan with almost hushed tones and reverence, while at the same time, Satan is bad. Although Paradise Lost also serves as a thought-provoking illumination of the biblical narrative of Satan's fall from glory and his corruption of mankind, it also serves as an allegorical tragedy to those who try to accomplish things without God's help.


In conclusion, Milton's view of an “epic” hero is one who tries to take glory away from God. By choosing Satan as the protagonist, he creates the ultimate “epic” hero. However, Satan's glory, like every other pagan hero, is temporary. Milton realizes that the real hero, Jesus, doesn't need to glorify himself when all of Creation is praising him. Instead, Jesus stoops to the level of a servant to take care of the needs of his people. Milton demonstrates through Satan's fall, and God's compassion, that Jesus is the better hero.
